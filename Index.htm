What did your Dad do during the war?

 

6145993 - Donald James Trinder (born 19th March 1917)

 

 

 

(Many of the photographs included below were found amongst his papers, however identical copies of some have also been found on the internet.

 Enlarged versions can be seen by clicking the relevant photo).

 

At the outbreak of war in September 1939, he was a 22 year old carpenter from Little Heath who had never seen the sea.

In March 1940, he enlisted in the 'East Surreys' and, following a period of training at Catterick, spent some time defending forward aerodromes in Kent. (Enlistment Letter).

 

Note - It is possible, that the East Surreys were used as a training regiment where newly enlisted men received their basic training before going to other regiments. There is an envelope addressed to his brother, Lionel Trinder who at the time of the letter, was also in the East Surreys. Later on Lionel went into the Royal Artillery.

The Rifle Brigade

Having transferred to the Rifle Brigade, he would have been based at their barracks in Winchester.

The Rifle Brigade was an old and well-respected regiment. Their training included teaching their long history and encouraging the feeling of belonging so, when in action they did not want to let their battalion or comrades down.

 

They were also taught to be responsible which led to the lower ranks being able to step into the shoes of their NCOs', when the need arose.

Later on, when operating in their 'flying' columns', it was said that everyone could do everyone else's job. With riflemen operating the guns etc

 

Unlike most regiments, the Rifle Brigade did not have colours (a flag) as a rallying point for the troops but used bugle calls instead. This tradition was based around the idea that their troops would be dispersed when in action.

They were also know for their 140 paces per minute, 'Quick March'.

 

In the beginning of May 1941 he had 7 days leave from 'B' Company, the 7th Battalion RB. On the rear of his leave pass is a ration card that he would have needed when he got home. (From the unused token on the card, it looks like he didn't use all his butter and cheese ration).(click here)

 

Must have been flush to have rolled such a fat one!

Going Overseas

At the end of July 1941, he embarked at either Liverpool or the Clyde for the passage to Africa as part of convoy WS10.

Convoy WS10 (Winston Special 10) was made up of 19 troop and cargo ships and a number of destroyer and cruiser escorts.

Having left the Clyde, to avoid the U-boats, it would have travelled West across the Atlantic until it was about 100 miles from the East Coast of America. Then South down the coast until it turned East again and back across the Atlantic to its first stop at Freetown.

 

After about 36 hours, taking on supplies it went on to South Africa where, owing to its size, it split with some ships going into Cape Town and others into Durban. Dad went into Durban where, during his stay, he was entertained to tea and cakes by the Baptist Church who, on the 16th September 1941, wrote to his family telling them he was OK. (Baptist Church Letter)

After a few days in Durban, many of the ships went on to Aden, where the convoy dispersed before going up the Suez canal.

The Durban ships in this convoy were Diomed, Indian Prince, Nea Hellas, Andes, Rangitiki, Orcades, Highland Monarch and Cameronia.

(More info on the convoy).

 

Note - WS10 is the most likely convoy as it left the Clyde on the 30th July, the date shown in his pay-book when he joined the MEF (Middle Eastern Force). Additionally, the date this convoy would have been in Durban, approximates to the date of the letter from the Durban Baptist Church (16th September 1941).

I remember him telling me that he slept on deck as below decks was really oppressive but at first light, the crew would hose everything down whether you were sleeping there or not. It was often the case that the troops did not know where they were going until arrival at their destination.

 

Geneifa

Bren Gun Carrier

(Library Photo)

 

On arrival in Suez (probably around the beginning of October 1941), he spent a number of weeks at Geneifa camp undergoing further training and acclimatisation. He had been trained with the rifle and Bren in England during June 1940 but undertook a course on Bren Gun Carriers at Geneifa in November 1941.

 

Whilst at the camp, his duties would have included sentry duty at the Italian POW camp and also at the hospital for wounded German soldiers.

Note - Geneifa was a large tented infantry camp located just outside Suez. It was around 15 miles long with each regiment having its own area.

 

There was very little entertainment at Geneifa apart from the NAFFI and Shafto's Cinema which showed old movies projected onto a white wall.

As the camp was close to the Suez Canal, some people went swimming in the lakes adjoining, (I can't see dad having done this).

 

In his pay-book, I could see nothing that related to unarmed combat training; however he certainly told me that he had undertaken this. It seems that the LRDG and SAS training camp was just down the road at Kabrit and it was not unusual for men from his battalion to go there for a few days to see if they were suitable (exiting from the rear of a truck whilst doing 30 mph was one of the things they learnt there).

 

 

BREN Gun

A licensed version of the Czechoslavak ZGB 33 light machine gun.

It took its name (BR) from Brno, the city where it was designed and (EN) from Enfield, the site of the Royal Small Arms factory where it was made.

I can remember him telling me how to kill a man with your bare hands; how to escape when held at gunpoint by a man standing behind you and how to tie a man so he was completely incapacitated, using only a boot lace. (When he showed me and I said 'you could break a mans arm doing this', his reply was 'we didn't trouble about things like that').

At the time, 60 years ago or more, I thought how useful it was knowing how to escape when held at gunpoint by a man standing behind you, however in all the years since, I have never used it – not even once.

 

It was usual for riflemen to stay from 2 to 6 weeks at Geneifa before being allocated to an under-strength battalion and then travel to the Western front by train. However, it seems from his pay-book, he underwent a Gas CAP test on the 22nd Dec 1941 and as this would mean going into a chamber, it is possible that he was still at Geneifa at that time.

The riflemen would look at the notice board every day to see if their name was on it and to

which battalion they had been allocated.

 

Note – At the end of 1941, the battalion was taken out of the line and on the 15th January 1942 started the move East, arriving at Beni Yusef camp (not far from Geneifa), on the 26th January 1942. So maybe he stayed at Geneifa waiting their return.

There was a large draft into the battalion on the 3rd March, so possibly he moved from Geneifa to the 2nd RB at Beni Yusef camp at this time. (His service record, when it finally arrives, should reveal the date he joins the 2nd battalion).

 

The Draft

When arriving at his unit (2nd battalion rifle brigade), he would have been allocated to whichever platoon was short of personnel. However, as he had been trained on carriers, he may have been designated to a scout or carrier platoon.

Note - From correspondence received after the war, it seems that at the time of his injury, he was in 'C' Company, 10 platoon but it is possible that he was with more than one platoon during his time with the battalion (It is very likely that the platoons were restructured when they went into Italy, as the battalion changed from a 'mobile unit' to hard infantry and the correspondence relates to the Italian campaign).

 

Albert Martin of 'A' and 'S' Companies in 'Hellfire Tonight' suggests that it was common for men to move between Sections, Platoons and even Companies. So the assumption that he was always in 'C' Coy, may not be correct.

Platoon - 3 Sections would make a platoon which would be led by an officer.

 

Company – in 'C' Company ('C' Coy), there were 4 platoons (9, 10, 11 and 12. One of these may have been a Carrier Platoon or Scout Platoon with about 12 Carriers (Bren Gun carriers or small armoured cars – they had a Scout and a Dingo). Another may have been a 'Mortar' platoon and another, a MG platoon (using water cooled Vickers).

 

Additionally the Company had a 6 truck Echelon responsible for supplying the sections with provisions and ammunition.

 

The support Company, 'S' Coy became an anti-tank company; originally armed with the ineffectual 2 pounder and later, the significantly better 6 pounder.

The 6 pounder really proved its worth in the famous 'Snipe' action at Kidney Ridge.

 

2nd Battalion Rifle Brigade

 

Comprising 5 Companys (A, B, C, the Support Company S and a large HQ Company), they were a motorised battalion mostly made up from men from North and East London although many of the officers had attended private schools such as Eton.

Whilst in the desert, the battalion was entirely self sufficient.

 

7th Motor Brigade

 

Logo of the 7th Armoured Division

(The Desert Rats)

At times, the 2nd Battalion Rifle Brigade were joined by the 7th RB, the KRRC (Kings Royal Rifle Corps), a troop of 25 pounders from the Royal Artillery, and some Bofur anti-aircraft guns, to form the 7th Motor Brigade (also called the 7th Motor Group or the 7th Support Group).

The primary task of this group was to act as a pivot for the 7th Armoured Division whereby they would prepare the ground for the tanks. This would involve clearing minefields, destroying ant-tank guns, dealing with infantry etc. However this group had many other tasks and were often used to protect the rear of the army when it was forced to withdraw.

Whilst associated with the 7th Armoured Division, the 2nd RB had the same logo as them, the desert rat.

 

Note – Desert Rat became a generic term for anyone serving in the North Africa campaign, however strictly speaking it should only have been used by the 7th Armoured Division and those units associated with them.

 

 

 

Flying ('Jock ') Columns

 

The Rifle Brigade was the original regiment used by 'Jock' Campbell to form his famous ''flying' columns and in 1942, the 2nd Battalion were used in the formation of 'March', 'April', 'July' and 'August' (the name given to the columns at that time).

These were small, highly mobile groups that included one or two companies of infantry, a couple of troops of RA 25 pounders and the RB's anti-tank guns. They worked well behind the enemy lines (up to 60 miles), harassing the enemy or monitoring their movements.

Their patrols, made up from members of different platoons, would be tasked with listening to the enemy and capturing enemy sentries who would be interrogated for information.

 

Note – Rifleman Martin wrote = ”Jock Campbell was a leader popular with all his troops, whose only shortcoming, from out point of view, was that he thought 2nd RB would be upset if they were left out of any action= ”.

 

Where was he in the Desert

 

The 2nd Battalion Rifle Brigade was an extremely mobile unit and went into action in many different guises. As a platoon, as a battalion, as part of the 7th Motor Brigade or as a company (or part of a company) involved in a 'Jock' Column.

So, unless the action was undertaken by the entire battalion, various parts of the battalion could be in 'action' at different places at the same time. Therefore, without knowing which Company, Platoon and Section he was in, it is very difficult to know where he was at any given time and the detail below is a combination of fact and considered assumptions.

 

The assumptions were based on the idea that at Kournine in March 1943, he was in 'C' Company (C Coy) and when injured in July 1944, he was also in 'C' Coy.

Additionally, a Corporal known as 'Pedlar' is mentioned in letters written to him after the war. 'Pedlar' Gardner is also mentioned a number of times in a book called 'Diary of a Desert Rat' which was written by a rifleman in 'C' Coy.

So it is assumed he was always in 'C' Coy.

 

At the time of his injury in Italy, it seems he was in 10 Platoon, however when the desert war was over and the battalion moved from North Africa to Italy, the majority of their transport was taken away and they were changed from a mobile motor battalion to 'hard infantry'. Instead of a Section truck, carrying 6 or 7 men and the items they needed on a day to day basis, they were transported in a lorry that carried the entire platoon, with other vehicles carrying their bedding etc. (They were allowed to keep a few Bren carriers).

This change from a mobile battalion to hard infantry, resulted in a re-structuring of Platoons, so whilst it seems he was in 10 Platoon when in Italy, it is not certain which he was in when in North Africa.

Note – I also have a recollection of him telling me that at some point, he was in the Kings Royal Rifles, although I have seen nothing to support this and my memory may be failing me. (The KRRC were part of the 7th Motor brigade, so this may have been what he meant).

Another recollection I have is or him telling me that they had been seconded to the LRDG (Long Range Desert Group who operated behind the lines and were often used to place the SAS into position). Again I have not found anything to confirm this but as he was involved in the 'Jock' columns, operating well behind enemy lines, this was likely.

 

Move to the Western Desert

 

On the 23rd / 24th March 1942, the pennant goes up on the command vehicle and they start the move forward to the western desert ('into the blue', being the term for going to the front). They were returning to the areas where they were successful in 1941.

Their route took them through Amirya, Alex, Mersa Matruth, Sidi Barani, Buqbuq, Sollum (El Salloun), Fort Capuzzo then the Western Desert.

 

On the 26th April, the 'Jock' columns were re-formed to monitor and harass the enemy

 

On the 27th April 1942 they become Operational (going to Rotonda Sengam).

 

Whilst in the desert, the 2nd RB were almost always in contact with the enemy. The actions detailed below are merely an example of what they were up to.

 

Life in the Desert

Imperial War Museum Photo of Infantry Truck

This would be home for a 'Section' of 6 men.

 

 

Life revolved around the section truck which was home for 6 men.

It carried their clothes, bedding, arms (Rifle, Bren, Boys anti-tank gun and ammunition), eating utensils and some rations along with a 'bail-out' box for each of the men.

Note - The 'bail-out' box carried their important personal effects (including their looted treasure) and in an emergency, could easily be picked up and carried to safety.

 

The windscreen and wing mirrors were removed from the 15cwt truck and anything that would reflect the sun was painted with oil and sand.

 

Generally they would sleep on the desert floor with only a blanket to keep them warm in the cold desert nights; always keeping an eye open for the scorpions which would creep into their bedding.

 

There would be a platoon cook but for their regular brew ups, they used a petrol can that was cut open, filled with sand and soaked in petrol. This was carried on the side of the truck and would allow a brew for 6 to be made in less than 2 minutes.

 

Maps of the desert were poor and navigation was undertaken at night by stars and by a sun compass (attached to the wing of the truck), during the day.

Compass points could be found using the sun and a watch.

 

A scruffy lot who look like they haven't washed behind their ears for a while. I think the man on the left is the person next to dad in the 'Cairo Zoo' photo (below) and there is another picture of the 'hard' one in dad's album. Is dad the one kneeling with his back to us?

Their diet mainly consisted of tinned M&V (Meat and Veg), tins of fruit, dried eggs etc along with biscuits that needed to be soaked in liquid before they could be eaten (biscuit burgoo).

I recall dad telling me, when they were near a river, they would supplement their diet by fishing, using grenades to stun the fish. This was borne out by Crimp in his 'Diary of a Desert Rat'.

 

Water was usually rationed at about 1 gallon per day per man but at times could be half a gallon or less. This ration was to cover everything from washing, eating and tea-making, with whatever was left, going into the radiators of their vehicles. The water often tasted of petrol and it was not uncommon, when water was short, for them to wash their clothes in petrol. The water was always warm.

 

Flies were a constant problem and it was usual to eat with one hand whilst trying to wave the flies away with the other. At one point, an order was issued that each man should kill 100 files each day but it made no difference. Any sort of injury would, immediately be covered in flies.

 

They received a ration of 50 cigarettes per man per week but these were locally made and thought to contain camel dung. They were supplemented by further supplies purchased from the NAAFI (Navy, Army and Air Force Institutes).

 

Desert sores were a common problem (resulting from any cut or bruise), they took a long time to heal and could result in a period in hospital. (I recall him telling me he suffered a bout of dysentery that put him in hospital).

 

During the khamsin season in spring, sandstorms would come without warning and prevent any fighting. They would reduce visibility to a few yards, make breathing and eating difficult and cover everything with sand. All they could do was to take cover and wait for it to end.

 

Note – the word 'khamsin' is the Arabic for fifty. The dry hot wind was called this because it equated to the number of days the sandstorm would appear.

 

 

Action in the Desert

 

Whilst in the desert, there would have been some form of contact with the enemy, virtually every day.

Patrols were a regular nightly activity and could take many forms including 'fighting' patrols which were undertaken to cause confusion and damage to the enemy and 'information gathering', where they might get as close to the enemy lines as possible and listen to what was going on, or capture a sentry on 'stag' for interrogation.

Apparently, he told Neil that on one patrol, they laid up in a 'wadi' (ravine or channel) with one person standing on another's shoulders and when an enemy sentry walked around the top, they grabbed him by his ankles and pulled him down into the gulley before taking him back for questioning.

 

Frequently they would attack columns of enemy transport by driving through them and shooting them up before leaving as quickly as they came.

 

For the more 'planned' attacks, they might go into 'Silent Action' where there was no preliminary barrage and it was necessary to remain quiet for as long as possible. In these circumstances, they would wear 'plimsolls' and leave their tin hat behind in case it was dropped.

Dad said that he always felt 'windy' when on the start line, waiting for the off but this passed as soon as they got going.

 

6 Pounder Anti-tank gun.

(Library Photo)

Their battalion included a number of anti-tank guns and when these were employed in a defensive role, the riflemen would dig slit trenches a few hundred yards in front of the guns, to protect them from infantry attack.

 

'S' Coy (Support Company) became the specialist anti-tank element of the battalion.

 

Rifleman Albert Martin, when writing about their daily active duties in 'Hellfire Tonight' said – = ”There was seldom a clearly definable front line where battle was joined or a defensive position that had to be breached to enable an advance to proceed. The reality was a series of battles taking place miles apart, with your own private conflict seemingly the only war going on a the time. Then your engagement was completed or broken off and you were sent to lend support in an action taking place some 10 miles away= ”.

 

 

 

 

 

Battle of Gazala

 

On the 9th May 1942, the battalion moved towards Tobruk where they took over the defence of the Al Aden box to protect the airfield ('C' Coy were tasked with protecting the ammunition dump).

From there they moved as part of the 7th motor brigade, to the Retma box, to undertake its defence.

Note – a number of so called 'boxes' were created along a defensive line. These boxes were manned by a brigade (a brigade was usually about 3 battalions) and were surrounded by minefields.

 

The Retma box was attacked by the enemy on the 25th May, as it passes to the north. It was only intended to delay the advance and, having caused much damage to the enemy, was overrun and evacuated.

 

At the end of May, the battalion moves to Sidi Rezegh and then towards Bir Hacheim, where the 'Free French' were surrounded by 10,000 enemy troops.

Earlier convoys trying to supply the encircled French had been completely destroyed and on the 6th June, 'C' coy is tasked with escorting a 40 lorry convoy to break through the enemy lines and supply the besieged troops.

With 11 Platoon leading and 10 Platoon covering the flanks, they drive straight through the enemy. However plans were changed and the first 15 vehicles, along with 11 Platoon, successfully continued into Bir Hacheim and the rear 25 vehicles turned back.

 

A couple of days later, 11 Platoon were able to break out of the box and a further attempt was made to get supplies in.

 

In early June, behind the enemy lines, around Bir Hacheim, they destroyed 100 MET (Mixed Enemy Transport), including guns and released 300 P.O.W's.

 

Free French troops after the evacuation of Bir Hacheim

On the 7th June 1942, they covered the 'Free French' break-out of 2,600 men and 1 woman (Susan Travers, an Englishwoman and the only female member of the French Foreign Legion) from Bir Hachiem and then picked up stragglers and ferried them to safety. They got into the box and formed a protective corridor through which the garrison could escape.

 

Martin wrote = ”What a bloody night it was! Several thousand troops, including our own men cooped up with them, were coming through a solid wall of fire. Our section was guarding them. It appeared to be a massacre. Dawn found hundreds of dazed, exhausted men, mostly wounded, just wandering around about ready to drop anywhere. We led them to our waiting trucks and ambulances and by 5 a.m. the wounded were well on the way. The final count was 2,700 men (and 1 woman) out of 3,600 were brought out safely= ”.

 

A couple of days after the Ben Hircheim breakout, the 'March', 'July' and 'August' columns combined and attacked 300 MET and 35 tanks, This action went on to the evening when the enemy withdrew.

Note – 'March' Column included 'C' Coy.

 

By the 19th June 1942, the 7th Motor Brigade (including 2 RB) is the only part of the 8th Army operating outside Tobruk. They were tasked with holding a gap open between the El Adem and Knightsbridge boxes so the garrison could break out. However this was not possible due to the lack of transport.

 

On the 21st June Tobruk fell. Dad told me he came out of Tobruk hanging on the side of an ambulance. He was challenged by a German motorcycle but allowed to proceed when he pointed to the Red Cross on the side of the vehicle.

 

For the next couple of weeks, they are protecting the rear of the retreating 8th army with 'S' coy guarding a minefield gap along the Siwa Track to Mersa Matruth. The enemy were using this gap to attack the rear of the retreating 8th Army forces.

 

On the 29th, the 7th Motor Brigade stop the 15th Panzer Division, allowing time for the Army to escape but this resulted in them being cut-off behind the Axis lines. Even so, they attack the rear of the Italian XX Corps and then continue to harass the enemy. They are the last element of the 8th Army to reach the Alamein Line.

 

By the middle of July 1942, the battalion are at Bir Hemeimet, at the Southern end of the Alamein line (North end of the Quattara Depression).

Battle of Alam El Halfa

At the end of August, during the battle of Alam El Halfa, the battalion delay the enemies advance and are in continuous action.

From the 3rd to the 12th of September 1942,  they create a 'forward defence line'.

 

Mid September, they go for a rest at Burg El Arab near Alex and from there to the Armoured Base Camp at Khathatba.

Amongst his papers are a receipt for a 5 day stay at 'Room 19 Victory House Hotel Cairo' and there are ticket stubs from the Metro Cinema on 4th Oct and Cinema Royal on 6th Oct.

It is assumed that these receipts were obtained during this period of leave.

2nd Battle of Alamein

On return from leave, the battalion had 2 weeks intensive training for their forthcoming role in the MTF (Mine Task Force), where they learnt how to probe for mines with a bayonet; although their primary task would be to protect the sappers doing the main mine clearing work.

 

The White Rhino

Logo of the 1st Armoured Brigade.

(Also known as the pregnant pig)

They were also required to change their logo from that of the Jeraboa (the Desert Rat symbol of the 7th Armoured Brigade) to the 'White Rhino'. For although they were still called the 7th Motor Brigade or Support Group, they had been moved from the 7th Armoured Brigade to the 1st Armoured Brigade, who were to play a significant part in the forthcoming battle

Their preparations included producing thousands of 'Star' lamps that were made by cutting a star shape into the side of a 'flimsie' (poorly made allied petrol can as opposed to the well made German 'Jerry' can).

 

On the first day of the battle (23rd October 1942), the 51st Highland division (infantry made up of Scottish battalions including the Black Watch) went through the minefields to the sound of bagpipes and attacked the enemy with rifle and bayonet. They were then followed by the 2nd RB who mopped up any enemy that had been by-passed.

The 2nd RB then helped and protected the sappers clearing the mines in an area know as the 'Star' gap.

Note – in the middle section of the attack, there were a number of minefields. The first was an Allied field and the rest were the enemies'.

There were 3 gaps to be created in these fields to allow the armour to pass through. They were to be called 'Sun', 'Moon' and 'Star'.

 

Once the minefields had been cleared, they marked them with the 'Star' lamps that they had previously made and were now illuminated by oil lamps placed inside. Their task was then to guide the 1st armoured division through these gaps.

 

Note - During Alamein, Oliver Leese was the commander in charge of XXX corps and later went on to command the 8th army in Italy.

Although not shown in his Wikipedia biography, for a time he lived in Welham Green (a couple of minutes walk from where I live now).

Had he known that a fellow desert rat lived just up the hill, I am sure he would have been a regular visitor at 70 Somers Road, chewing the fat and smoking a roll-up or two.

 

Snipe Action (part of the 2nd Battle of Alamein)

The Snipe Action by Terence Cuneo

 

After 3 days clearing the minefields at Alamein, where they had little or no rest, the battalion of less than 300 (including support), were tasked with providing a pivot at Kidney Ridge, that would be used by the armour on the following day. There followed 36 hours of intense action where they were attacked by around 100 Axis tanks and guns which resulted in them destroying between 50 and 80 of them.

In addition to the attacks by the enemy, they also suffered 3 bouts of friendly fire which caused many casualties.

For the battalion's action at Snipe, their Colonel (Turner) was awarded the Victoria Cross. (He always said it was on behalf of the battalion).

 

This destruction of around 10% of Rommels armour, made a significant contribution to the final outcome of the battle of El Alamein and the desert war in general. (For a detailed description of the Snipe Action, click here)

 

Note – at the outset of this action, there had been a disagreement between the 1st Armoured Division and the Royal Artillery as to their exact position. In fact this disagreement amounted to a difference of more than half a mile and, along with the loss of the RA FOO (Forward Observation Officer), may have resulted in their 3 bouts of 'friendly' fire.

 

When on the start line, they had been attacked by an enemy bomber which resulted in a number of casualties. The medical staff and ambulances stayed on the 'start line' to help them and were then, due to heavy fire, unable to move forward. So, when in action in the 'Snipe' position, they had no medical officer or ambulance in attendance.

 

A couple of days after the Snipe Action at Kidney Ridge, they were again asked to service a minefield gap for tanks and for some of their carriers to guide the tanks through the gaps. Having accompanied the tanks through the fields, at first light they retired.

Of the 52 tanks they guided through, only 6 survived.

 

On to Tunisia

 

In early 1943, their vehicles were taken to be used for other purposes, so the battalion was taken out of the line whilst the Axis forces were pushed back towards Tunisia.

 

In early March 1943 the Americans had joined the war and their 'Green' troops were fighting in Tunisia.

The fighting was not going well so the 2nd Battalion RB start the moved to the front which was now more than 1500 miles away.

They went through Baracca – Mersa Brega – El Aghelia – Buerat El Hsun – Ben Ulid – Tarhuna then Castel Benito (Near Tripoli) – Skirted around Tripoli and crossed the frontier into Tunisia – Ben Gardane – Passed through Medenine and set up camp in the hills.

 

On the 20th March 1943 they were due to take part in the attack on Mareth, supposedly going through to Gabes when the Northumbrian Infantry made a penetration. However the penetration was not made. So a couple of days later they went south to support the New Zealanders.

 

At the end of March they arrived in the Matmata Mountains

 

Djebel Bou Kournine, Tunisia

Bou Kournine

After a number of attacks by other units had been unsuccessful, on the 29th March, 'C' Coy were asked to take a hill called Djebel Bou Kournine,

The hill was well defended and littered with booby traps along with the dead of previous assaults.

 

It did not go well.

 

After the company runner had been killed, although they were under very heavy machine gun and small arms fire, he was asked by the Company Commander to carry messages to various groups of men.

He told me that, although it was at night and very dark, he was able to find all of these groups. He felt that a greater power had helped him.

For his bravery under fire and the part he played in the safe extraction of his company, he was awarded the Military Medal (Citation).

 

 

 

The citation was signed by – Lieutenant Colonel Pearson (2nd RB), Brigadier Bosville (Commander 7th Motor Brigade), General Lumsden (Commander in Chief of Middle East Forces), Major General Briggs (Commander 1st Armoured Division), Lieutenant General Horrocks (Commander 9th Corps and later better known as presenter of the BBC television series 'World at War').

 

Crimp wrote, = ”The uproar continues with hardly a break. But time passes quickly under the cold starlight and by two o'clock no news has come in. Soon things get rather more fierce. Mortaring starts in earnest,, crashes and brief red bursts glow on the upper slopes. Red and green flares rise from about half way up and the 2 in command grows quite excited, surmising the success signal, But 'success signal my foot!= ” is the opinion of the rest of us, for whenever the geens and reds go up, or rather and exactly regular number of seconds after, salvos of heavy shells – probably 105mm – roar through the air like express trains from somewhere over in the hindward mountains and land with a shattering, scattering crashes on the slopes of Bou Kournine.

By half past three, the situations quietened down but still no signal's been received. Men however have begun drifting back in small groups,, most of them badly shaken. The talk of crawling up sheer precipices to find every level expense swept by machine gun fire, of wounded rolling all the way to the bottom; of tripwire mines and booby-traps on handgrip ledges; of areas automatically illuminated on approach by flares and bulbs, immediately drawing fire, of premature rifle-shots from stumbling men, supposed to have given the game away; and walls and caves and all sorts of cunning defensive devices on the summit, piled round with the dead of previous assaults= ”.

 

An extract from 'Hellfire Tonight;, written by Rifleman Albert Martin of 'S' Company, observing the attacking 'C' Coy men returning from Bou Kournine after the action.

= ”In the early hours the firing diminished. Groups of exhausted men made their way back to us, drawn faces, sagging shoulders and an unnatural shine in the eyes graphically conveying the trauma of the last 3 hours. The booby traps had been cleverly placed at every access route, trip wires all but invisible, setting off fixed line machine gun fire; the bodies from previous attacks still lying where they fell.= ”

Later, when the enemy had left the position, the company returned to the hill to bury their dead. They found that the defences were impregnable and although now in daylight, a number of the burial party became casualties to the booby traps which had made it so difficult for the night attackers of 'C' Coy.

The position was by-passed and the enemy later withdrew. It was never actually taken.

 

At the end of March, they moved to El Hamma and during April and May continued through Le Ksous – Plain of Goubellaiat – El Aroussa – Sidi Mahdi - Creteville – Grombalia and then to the sea, 12 miles from Tunis.

Greek Mutiny

In 1941, when the Germans overran Greece, many Greek troops were extricated and sent to Egypt. These were formed into an infantry brigade.

In April 1944, these troops, many of whom were communist sympathisers, mutinied. They shot some of their officers, who were still loyal to the King of Greece and imprisoned the rest. They were fully armed and had been ready to go to Italy.

In response to the mutiny, the 7th armoured division, recalled their tanks (they had previously been handed back ready for the divisions re-deployment) and along with the Rifle Brigade and 14th/20th Hussars (green troops with no battle experience), surrounded the Greek encampment.

By this time the Greek troops had taken possession of some high ground overlooking the camp.

The Rifle Brigade undertook the recognisance of the camp and when it was decided to take the high ground from the Greeks, the RB were tasked with this.

The taking of the hill was accomplished by a Company from the RB but their Company Commanding Officer was killed in this action.

 

There followed a stalemate and a parley where the Greeks asked for food (as the main water supply line passed through their camp, they were never short of water). The response from Brigadier Otto Palmer (and erratic man straight out of England and again with no battle experience), was to tell them that if they did not surrender, he would send the tanks in.

The troops then surrendered. They were disarmed and send into prison camps where it is understood, the conditions were very harsh.

 

On the 28/04/1944, he moved from MEF (Middle Eastern Force), to CMF (Combined Mediterranean Force – combined with the Americans)

 

Instead of returning to the UK, the Rifle Brigade would then go to Italy instead of the Greeks.

 

Lt Gen Sir Brian Horrocks wrote than he had :

= ”come to the conclusion that the 'Black Buttons' and the Rifle Brigade in particular, are probably the best infantry regiment serving in any army in the world= ”.

 

 

Italy (to be expanded and sorted out, when more research undertaken)

 

The battalion arrived in Taranto, Italy in early May and was equipped with new vehicles.  They then spent a couple of weeks preparing for battle on the Adriatic front.

However, 12 hours before being committed, they were ordered to Capua, North of Naples and on arrival on the 25th May 1944, the vehicles that made the 2nd battalion rifle brigade so mobile and effective in the desert war, were taken away. They were left with a few carriers and along with the 7th and 10th battalions, became the' 61st lorried infantry.

 

Instead of the section truck which in the desert had been driven by one of their own and was home to 6 men, they were transported by the RASC (Royal Army Service Corps) in a 3 tonner that carried an entire Platoon.

They were no longer a fully independent mobile fighting force but a 'hard' infantry unit associated with the 6th armoured division.

 

On the 29th May they left Capua and joined the brigade column, which joined the division column which joined the corps column.

They were now in a convoy thought to be around 20 miles long moving at 5 mph which due to the bombing and mining of the main road, was moving along country lanes.

 

 

Perugia

 

 

Monte Lignano

Early July 1944 saw the 2nd RB having a bad time on the slopes of Monte Lignano.

The 1st RB was on located close by on Monte Maggio, where following a night battle by the 1st RB, their stretcher bearers had teamed up with the enemy's and after they had picked up their wounded, they all sat down together for a smoke.

 

On the 9th July, the 2nd RB were withdrawn from Lignano and replaced by a New Zealand battalion who on the 14th, were to attack both Lignano and Maggio.

Prior to the attack there was a barrage made up of 25 pounders and 4.2= ” mortars that lit up the mountains with shells landing at the rate of 20 to 30 per second. It lasted from an hour after nightfall until 1:00 am in the morning during which time there were more shells fired than in the opening barrage of Alamein.

 

 

4.2= ” Mortar known to the enemy as 'Whispering Death' as its shell could not be heard in flight.

PIAT Anti Tank Gun

(Projectile Infantry Anti Tank)

 

 

 

 

 

PIAT

I recall dad telling me about the PIAT and how dangerous it was.

 

The PIAT, using 'shaped charge' ammunition, was introduced in 1943 as a replacement for the BOYS anti-tank rifle which, after an investigation, was found not to have seriously damaged any tanks in North Africa.

 

The gun incorporated a large spring which would launch the bomb and ignite the propellant. Unlike the Bazooka, it did not have a back blast which would disclose the whereabouts of the user to the enemy.

 

It required a tremendous effort to compress the spring and load it for the first firing. However, having fired once, it used the recoil to compress the spring and arm it for subsequent firings. It would be carried into action, already primed but without the bomb loaded.

 

The use of 'shaped charge' ammunition made it extremely effective against almost all enemy armour.

 

 

The End of the 2nd Battalion

In December 1944, the battalion attacked a small village called Tossignano and were badly beaten up.

Their casualties amounted to 220 men. Of these, 23 were killed, 60 were wounded and evacuated during the battle and the rest were taken prisoner, many of whom were wounded.

In January 1945 the battalion was broken up with the 2nd name taken over by one of the territorial battalions.
